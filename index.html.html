Supported by UNICEF & the Committee on the Rights of the Child
the heart of our week
What it is
A promise by countries to protect the rights of every child under 18 — whoever they are, wherever they live.
Why it exists
Rights mean nothing if children never know they have them. The Convention asks grown-ups to actively share it — and to let children use these rights as they grow.
Why it's ours this week
Its own words: all rights are connected, equal, and can't be taken away. A festival is where children get to put them into practice.

What you do, Monday morning

Four moves that turn a contest into practice.

The content each child makes is theirs, shaped by you. These moves are how a child actually exercises a right, not just hears its name.

01

Let the content be theirs

Keep the subject open within the contest — their words, their art, their idea.
they practisethe right to share freely what they think
02

Welcome every way in

Let them take part by speaking, drawing, writing, building — or watching first.
they practiseexpressing "in any other way"
03

Make room for every culture

Welcome work in home languages and from different cultures — and celebrate that difference openly.
they practisetheir right to their culture & language
04

Honour the brave try

Name a strength before any critique; cheer courage as loud as the prize.
they practisebeing safe to take a risk

light safeguards

A child only practises freely when they feel safe. None of these add real work — they make protection something children can feel.

A shared pledge. Write the festival agreement together on day one.
Take part by choice. Watcher, maker or leader — each is respected.
Everyone included. Every child has a place, whatever their language or background.
Briefed adults. A 15-minute huddle: how to listen, how to notice.
A calm corner. One quiet space, one kind adult, no fuss.
An easy way to speak up. A worry box, or a word with any adult.
Kind words first. A strength named before any suggestion.
